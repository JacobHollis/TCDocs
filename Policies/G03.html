# G03 - Contingency Planning

This document forms part of Tideway Care’s policy Framework and should be read in accordance with the following other policies:

Policy Reference Policy Name
G01 Governance, Quality & Performance
OP01 Safeguarding Adults and Children
OP02 Risk Management
OP03.1 Serious Incidents

# Scope and Application of this Policy

The policy applies to all employed staff working in all services provided by Tideway Care; specifically, this is anyone with a contract of employment with Tideway Care.

The policy lays out the areas for which we have contingency plans in place to seek prevention, early intervention, action and follow up responses and recovery when faced with an emergency situation.

This policy does not include responses to medical emergencies which require an immediate response for the people we support. Emergency procedures are however in place to support staff to act swiftly in response to a medical emergency, see policy OP09 – Physical Health for further details.

# Legal & Regulatory Context

This policy has been produced with reference to the following legislation, guidance and directives;

  • The NHS Emergency Planning Guidance 2005
  • The Civil Contingencies Act (CCA) 2004
  • Beyond A Major Incident 2004
  • British Standard BS 25999 (Parts 1 & 2)
  • Health & Safety at Work Act 1974
  • Management of Health and Safety at Work Regulations 1999

# Equality Statement

In the implementation of this policy, Tideway Care will not discriminate against any colleague or customer on grounds of their race, ethnic origin, gender, sexuality, marital status, disability, age, religion or class.

# Policy Statement

This policy requires Tideway Care Senior Management Team to ensure there are systems and processes in place to respond to an emergency situation. The policy covers the following potential emergency situations:

  • Fire, flood, extreme weather conditions
  • Loss of utilities, including IT and telephone systems
  • Major disruptions to staffing e.g. as result of an epidemic, transport disruption, industrial accident, inability to recruit
  • Major accident or incident, national disaster, terrorist attack

# Policy Outcome

After reading this policy you will be able to:

  • Understand the importance of contingency planning and practice drills
  • Know where to go for help in emergency situations
  • Access the resources and plans you need to maintain the continuity of services

# 1. Introduction

1.1 The continued safety and wellbeing of the people we support and our staff are a priority and this policy supports the ongoing ability to safeguard people from harm as a result of a major event or emergency that damages systems, reduces capacity in our staffing resource or impacts on the environment the people we support live in, these will be referred to as emergency situations.

1.2 The purpose is of the policy to establish and implement procedures, emergency drills and back up support for responding to an emergency situation.

1.3 We have a contingency plan in place for responding to emergency situations which is frequently reviewed and amended as necessary to assist in responding, managing and reviewing an emergency situation. The documented plan is called Tideway Care Service Contingency Plan.

# 2. Contingency planning

2.1 Every service will require their own contingency plans to respond to a major incident or emergency situation.

2.2 The contingency plan must be updated as required and fully reviewed annually as a minimum. Team Managers are responsible for ensuring contingency plans are complete and up to date.

2.3 Contingency planning, scenario planning, drills or back up resources checks must be undertaken for every service as follows:

Major Incident/Emergency Action Frequency
Fire Fire Drills Bi-Monthly
Power Cut Check generator / back up power Monthly
Staff Shortages Check contingency plans Monthly
Hostage Situation Scenario planning exercise Quarterly
IT Backup Systems Check back up Monthly
Major Accident Nearby Scenario planning exercise Quarterly
Extreme Weather Conditions Scenario planning exercise Quarterly

2.4 Each of the above checks must be recorded and any lessons learnt should be shared, disseminated and used to update any contingency plans as appropriate.

2.5 Remember: CARE

Communicate – ensure everyone knows about the contingency plans

Appraise – get the team to help refine the plan through review, scenarios and drills

Review – revisit the plan, can it be improved?

Enhance – tweak the plan so that it now reflects what you have learnt from driving the CAR.

2.6 The Senior Management team have in place a system for checking all necessary contingency plans are in place, checks completed as above and reviewed annually as a minimum.

# 3. Preparing your contingency plans

3.1 The following are important to note when contingency planning:

  • Risk assess the damage- have you identified how you will assess the risk in the context of the service accommodation and the people who we support living there?
  • Clarify the possible scenarios - What could happen? / What will you do in response? / What can the team do in advance to prepare
  • Consider the backup resources you will require given the different scenarios – management support, communications, financial resources, coordination, logistical and technical responses
  • Prioritise the risk – if there are several risks in relation to a scenario; have you considered how you will rank them and prioritise?
  • Develop a timeline for how the scenario may unfold - practice an order of action to address most urgent and highest risk rated areas first.
  • Consider factors that will impact positively on others - what will be most important actions to get your service operational again?

3.2 Remember prevention is key to contingency planning – what needs putting in place to prevent a major incident? E.g. fire safety precautions, staffing bank and agency resources, staff training and awareness sessions, etc.

# 4. Loss or damage to premises

4.1 Remember to think about the following:

  • Evacuation requirements
  • Emergency repair requirements
  • Less urgent repair requirements
  • Utilities requirements
  • New furniture and furnishing requirements
  • Temporary accommodation requirements

4.2 Mitigating factors may include fire safety precautions / increasing staffing when someone has an increased need for support / responsive repairs, so damage doesn’t escalate / etc.

# 5. Loss of key staff

5.1 In most cases any unplanned staff changes can be covered in house or by a trusted bank/agency. Agency use is utilised as a last resort as we know that quality of support delivery can be reduced by high agency staff use.

5.2 In the case of a major incident occurring where there is a brief or prolonged shortage of staffing the Team Managers will need to refer to the contingency plan to ensure safe staffing levels are in place.

# 6. Loss of IT / Data

6.1 The core objectives of contingency planning include the capability to:

  • Restore operations at an alternate site (if necessary)
  • Recover operations using alternate equipment (if necessary)
  • Perform some or all of the affected business processes using other means

6.2 The contingency plan must address IT system components such as:

  • Local, wide area and wireless networks including Internet access
  • Server systems such as file, application, print and database
  • Web sites
  • Security systems such as firewalls, authentication servers, and intrusion detection
  • Desktop, laptop, PDA systems as back up resources

6.3 Contingency plans must continue to account for the provisions of the General Data Protection Regulations (See G05 - Data Protection) where personal information is concerned. For example, if paper records/ notebooks etc are used as an interim solution; the physical security and access control of those records must be controlled appropriately.

# 7. Loss of Telecommunications

7.1 Telephone networks are known to fail at times and our services rely on good communication. With mobile phones and other technologies communication can be restored by using different methods. However; it is important to check which media is acceptable and safe to use as a contingency and also consider if the alternative network, internet / data services are also unavailable how you would manage communications.

# 8. Loss of Utilities

8.1 A record of all utility company contact details for emergency situations on the Service Contingency Plan.

8.2 All emergency contact details should be updated on the plan should the service provider change.

# 9. Loss of a Key Partner or Supplier

9.1 The plan should consider the impact of losing a key partner or a major supplier that the service relies on. Ensure there are alternative arrangements available should the need arise.

9.2 The plan should also cover a change in a key supplier and have a transition plan in place when changing over.

# 10. Disruption Due to a Fuel Shortage

10.1 It is possible that there will be shortages of diesel fuel for instance as they phase out diesel cars and demand starts to decline; providers may stop providing diesel. Or unleaded petrol may be in short supply due to an oil rig disaster or the national grid may go down and electric cars cannot be charged. The plan should consider the possibilities and how the service be able to ensure continuity and enable staff to pool resources to get to work

# 11. Disruption Due to Severe Weather

11.1 For adverse weather conditions you will need to assess risks in terms of staffing, stocks and supplies, access to services the people we support benefit from, etc. Ensure you have a plan in place for each eventuality and put in place strategies to reduce potential disruption.

# 12. Other disruption

12.1 Other possible disruptions must be considered and a plan in place to ensure there is minimal impact on the people we support and staff. When creating contingency plans it may not be possible to anticipate every scenario that should be planned for, however efforts should be made to cover as many risks as possible.
