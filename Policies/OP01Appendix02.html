# OP01: Appendix 2 - Information on types of abuse and what to look for to spot potential abuse

Appended to:

# OP01 - Safeguarding Adults and Children

# Physical Abuse

# What this might mean

  • The use of force that results in pain or injury or a change in the child or adult’s natural physical state, including assault, hitting, slapping, pushing, and kicking
  • Withholding or misuse of medication
  • Unlawful or inappropriate use of restraint or physical interventions and/or deprivation of liberty is physical abuse.
  • Confining or locking someone in one room
  • Denying access to transport

# What to look out for

  • Unexplained injuries
  • Injuries inconsistent with the lifestyle of the vulnerable adult
  • Fractures
  • Sprains
  • Dislocations
  • Lacerations
  • Black eyes
  • Scalds/cigarette burns/friction burns/electrical appliance burns
  • Pressure sores
  • Welt marks
  • Bruises (especially in well protected areas)
  • Drowsiness, confusion due to over-sedation
  • Delays in seeking medical attention
  • Anxiety or fear more evident in the presence of the abuser
  • Dehydration
  • Physical Restraint
  • Physical sanctions
  • A lock on the outside of a room
  • A physical environment that does not allow access to other parts of the home

# Domestic Abuse

# What this might mean

This is abuse by someone who is or has been an intimate partner or family member and includes:

  • Psychological abuse
  • Physical abuse
  • Sexual abuse
  • Financial abuse
  • Emotional abuse
  • "Honour‟ based violence
  • Female Genital Mutilation
  • Forced marriage

Includes the abuse of someone aged 16 or more Includes abuse by family members that are not partners Includes abuse from partners in same sex relationships

# What to look out for

Domestic abuse is often identified through other abuse types such as physical, psychological etc, so the signs to look out for will be included in many other sections in this guide. In addition, signs could also include:

  • Incident or pattern of incidents of controlling, coercive or threatening behaviour
  • Absences from work/education notified by partner/family member
  • Seeking approval from partner/family member for activities someone would ordinarily agree to themselves
  • Use of words by the individual suggesting they are being controlled such as saying “THEY let me‟, “THEY allowed me‟
  • Continually inconvenient to meet or talk to people in person

# Sexual Abuse

# What this might mean

This is the involvement of a child or vulnerable adult in sexual activities or relationships which are for the gratification of the other person and which either :

  • They do not want and have not consented to; or
  • They cannot understand and are not able to consent to.

This includes:

  • Rape
  • Sexual assault or harassment
  • Fondling/ inappropriate looking or touching
  • Offensive or suggestive language/innuendo Being photographed / filmed
  • Subjection to pornography or witnessing sexual acts
  • Indecent exposure

# What to look out for

  • Changes in behaviour (e.g., more withdrawn, depressed, confused, fearful, agitated)
  • Reluctance to be alone with an individual known to them
  • Unexplained difficulty in walking or sitting; torn bloody or stained underclothes
  • Pain or itching in the genital area
  • Bruising or bleeding in external genitalia vaginal or anal areas
  • Venereal disease
  • Sexualised behaviour

# Psychological Abuse

# What this might mean

This is behaviour that has a harmful effect on a child or vulnerable adult’s emotional health and development. This includes:

  • Harassment
  • Shouting and swearing
  • Insulting and humiliation
  • Intimidation and coercion
  • Ignoring
  • Blaming
  • Lack of stimulation
  • Cyber bullying
  • Threats of harm or abandonment
  • Depriving an individual of the right to choice, information and privacy
  • Deprivation of contact
  • Isolation from religious or cultural activities or antipathy to a religion or cultural activity
  • Unreasonable and unjustified withdrawal of services or supportive networks

Behaviour which can be deliberately linked to causing serious psychological and emotional harm may constitute a criminal offence. Specialist advice from the Police should be sought.

# What to look out for

  • Fear
  • Depression
  • Withdrawal Passivity
  • Confusion
  • Low self-esteem
  • Running away
  • Unusual weight loss
  • Disturbed sleep pattern
  • Change in appetite
  • Loss of independence
  • Failure to engage within the wider community Preventing access of other people to the home

# Financial and Material Abuse

# What this might mean

This is the use of a child or vulnerable adult's property, assets, income without their informed consent, or making financial transactions which they do not comprehend unless this is legally sanctioned. This includes:

  • Taking possessions
  • Stealing or misappropriating money
  • Using pressure to obtain rights to property
  • Pressure to give money away
  • Fraud
  • Internet scamming

# What to look out for

  • Change in living conditions
  • A “disappearing” pension/ benefits
  • Frequent requests for payment of outstanding bills
  • Sudden and/ or large withdrawal from bank etc.
  • Lack of heating, clothing or food
  • Power of Attorney obtained when adult is unable to comprehend and give consent Assumption of mental incapacity without assessment
  • Adult managing financial affairs is evasive or uncooperative
  • Unexplained disappearance of financial documents e.g. building society/ bank books
  • Recent addition of authorised signers on a customer’s account
  • Sudden or unexpected changes in a will or other financial documents
  • Sudden befriending and accompanying provision that coincides with benefit entitlement payments

# Modern Slavery

# What this might mean

This includes:

  • Slavery
  • Human trafficking
  • Forced labour
  • Domestic servitude

Traffickers and slave masters use whatever means they have at their disposal to coerce, deceive and force individuals into a life of abuse, servitude and inhumane treatment.

# What to look out for

  • Difficulty paying bills despite long working hours
  • Inability to leave a job as they are paying off a debt (sometime the fee for being brought to the country)
  • Lack of awareness of working rights such as work time directive, minimum wage etc
  • Employer provides accommodation that is often overcrowded, not regulated or contracted

# Discriminatory

# What this might mean

This is abuse, including harassment, bullying, slurs or similar treatment based on a person’s:

  • age
  • disability
  • gender reassignment
  • marriage and civil partnership
  • pregnancy and maternity
  • race
  • religion or belief
  • sex
  • sexual orientation

# What to look out for

  • Lack of respect shown to an individual
  • Signs of sub-standard service offered to an individual
  • Repeated exclusion from rights afforded to others such as health, education, employment, criminal justice
  • Unequal selection process or barring from accessing services, leisure opportunities specific to their social, cultural and religious backgrounds
  • Inappropriate use of language
  • Inappropriate language and behaviour towards the vulnerable adult is not challenged
  • The vulnerable adult appears isolate

# Organisational

# What this might mean

This includes neglect and poor care practice within a formal care setting or in relation to care provided in someone’s own home.

This may take the form of:

  • Isolated incidents of poor practice or unsatisfactory care practice or
  • Ongoing ill-treatment

It can be as a result of the structure, policies, processes and practices within an organisation

# What to look out for

  • No flexibility in bed time and/or deliberate waking
  • Inappropriate medical or nursing procedures
  • Lack of personal clothing and possessions
  • Lack of individual, adult centred care
  • Unhomely or stark living or day care areas
  • Inappropriate confinement or restriction
  • Lack of stimulation and choice
  • Lack of privacy in personal care e.g. toileting, bathing , washing, dressing, editing mail, restricting visitors
  • Staff using master keys without due cause
  • Staff entering rooms or flats without permission
  • Breaches of confidentiality
  • Restrictive practices in the use of communal facilities

# Neglect and Acts of Omission

# What this might mean

Omission This is behaviour that results in the child or vulnerable adult's basic needs not being met and includes:

  • Ignoring medical, emotional or physical care needs
  • Failure to provide adequate food/drink
  • Failure to administer medication
  • Inappropriate administering of medicine
  • Failure to provide a safe and adequately heated environment
  • Failure to assist with appropriate levels of hygiene
  • Failure to provide access to appropriate health, social care or educational services

Neglect Can be intentional or unintentional. Unintentional neglect could result from a carer failing to meet the needs of the adult at risk for instance because they do not understand their needs or know about services available.

# What to look out for

  • Dehydration
  • Malnutrition
  • Unexplained failure to respond to prescribed medication
  • Infections
  • Pressure sores
  • Inadequate clothing
  • Hypothermia
  • Clothing in poor condition
  • Untreated injuries or medical problems
  • Poor personal hygiene
  • Failure to respond to social interaction

# Self-Neglect

# What this might mean

This covers a wide range of behaviour covering someone’s neglect of their own:

  • Personal hygiene
  • Health
  • Surroundings
  • Self neglect can be intentional, such as when a person makes an active choice to not take medication.

Self neglect can be unintentional, such as when a person’s health related condition results in an inability to act. Care should be taken to consider whether neglect is genuinely caused by the individual’s actions (or lack of action) or by others

# What to look out for

  • Hoarding items and pets
  • Poor physical living conditions
  • Neglecting household maintenance
  • Living in an unclean environment
  • Poor personal hygiene
  • Unkempt appearance
  • Failure/unwillingness to take medication or to adhere to medical treatments and appointments
  • Inappropriate dress for the weather conditions
  • An unawareness of living conditions or personal circumstances

# Exploitation by radicalisers

# What this might mean

Exploitation by radicalisers who promote violence involves the exploitation of susceptible people in order to draw them into violent extremism.

In July 2015, the Counter Terrorism and Security Act 2015 came into force creating a statutory duty on public bodies to have due regard to the need to prevent people from being drawn into terrorism. The Counter Terrorism and Security Act 2015 makes the ‘Channel Panel’ a legal requirement.

‘Channel’ is a multi-agency safeguarding programme providing tailored support to people who have been identified as at risk of being drawn into terrorism. The support offered can come from any of the partners on the Panel which includes the local authority, police, education and health providers.

The person’s engagement in the programme is voluntary at all stages. Guidance on ‘Prevent’ can be found can be found here

# What to look out for

  • Being overly secretive about their online viewing – this being one of the core ways in which ISIS is known to communicate.
  • Displaying feelings of isolation or expressions of an ‘us and them’ mentality – a sign of the sense of social isolation.
  • Becoming more argumentative or domineering in their viewpoints, being quick to condemn those who disagree and ignoring views that contradict their own.
  • Questioning their faith or identity.
  • Downloading or promotion extremist content.
  • Social isolation – losing interest in activities they used to enjoy, distancing themselves from friends and social groups.
  • Altered appearance – change in style of dress and/or personal appearance.
  • Abnormal routines, travel patterns or aspirations.
