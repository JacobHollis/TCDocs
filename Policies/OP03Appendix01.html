# OP03: Appendix 1 - Categorisation of Serious Incidents

Appended to:

# OP03 - Incidents

A serious incident can be identified through applying a set of factors to an incident and requires a different response that those not categorised as serious. All unexpected deaths are regarded as serious. The following factors will determine the likelihood of the incident being serious:

  1. The level of harm is graded as medium to high
Grading Description Examples
None/Near Miss Any incident that had the potential to cause harm but was prevented or any incident that ran to completion, but no harm caused Person was going to assault another person, but staff intervened and prevented them gaining access to the person.

A medication chart was written up wrong and someone noticed and got it amended. Had they not noticed the medication given would likely cause harm to the person receiving the medication.
Low Any incident that caused harm, but no medical intervention required Scratches, minor bruising etc

Small fire created in a rubbish bin but put out by person with a cup of water.
Medium Any incident that resulted in significant injury requiring medical intervention Bleeding profusely as a result of an assault - Ambulance called

Fell off the wall in the garden and in great pain in left leg
High Any incident that appears to have resulted in any permanent harm Person saws off their finger making cabinets for the office - Loss of limb

Person jumps from a height leaving them with broken legs and possibly going to not retain mobility.
Death Unexpected death of person Person found dead in bedroom thought to have been in good health
  1. The Tideway Care Policies are breached
  2. Our legal duty to protect is breached and therefore prosecution could occur
  3. There is an emerging theme that suggests several near misses / low level incidents of the same pattern may be indicating a hidden issue that constitutes an investigation.
  4. Leads to a person requiring medical treatment in hospital
  5. Emergency services are required to respond and deal with incident
  6. Leads to a safeguarding report where actual harm has occurred
  7. Meets the NHS criteria for a Serious Incident that Requires Investigation (SIRI)

# Serious Incident Examples

# Documentation / Administration

Types: Clerical errors, MHA errors, confidentiality breaches, data protection errors, loss of personal information, loss of equipment holding personal data

Example: Document with personal details of people we support left on the train.

Factors

  • A breach of our legal duty to maintain confidentiality
  • A breach of TC Data Protection Policy
  • People we support are requesting investigation and seeking legal advice

# Equipment, machinery or hazardous substances

Types: Medical device failures, equipment failure, transport vehicle incidents, hazardous substances

Example: Electrical fault leading to a person being electrocuted and has a burn requiring medical treatment in hospital

Factors

  • A breach of our legal duty to maintain safety
  • A breach of the TC Health and Safety Policy
  • Leads to a person requiring medical treatment in hospital
  • Leads to a safeguarding report

# Accidents

Types: Including slips, trips, falls, scalding, etc

Example: Person falls over a chair left in the doorway holding open a fire door and causes bruising. This occurs three times in a week despite managers asking staff to cease this practice at once.

Factors

  • A breach of our legal duty to maintain safety
  • A breach of the TC Health and Safety Policy
  • There is an emerging theme that suggests several near misses / low level incidents of the same pattern may be indicating a hidden issue that constitutes an investigation.

# Missing

Types: Person not present as expected, not returning from leave, unknown whereabouts

Example: Person went to shops alone and usually takes 90 minutes as they met their mother in a café near the shops. They have not returned in the past 4 hours and mother says she left them 3 hours ago having had an argument about a holiday.

Factors

  • Our legal duty to protect may be breached and therefore prosecution could occur.
  • Emergency services are required to respond and deal with incident – i.e. reported missing to police

# Self-Harm

Types: Attempted suicide, all self inflicted injury or attempted injury

Example: Person tied a ligature around their neck suspended by the curtain and was found unconscious

Factors

  • The level of harm is categorised as medium to high
  • The Tideway Care Policies are breached
  • Our legal duty to protect is breached and therefore prosecution could occur
  • Leads to a person requiring medical treatment in hospital
  • Emergency services are required to respond and deal with incident
  • Leads to a safeguarding report where actual harm has occurred
  • Meets the NHS criteria for a serious incident that requires investigation (SIRI) – a never event

# Violence

Types: Actual physical person to person contact with intent to harm, (whether staff, service user or visitor)

Example: Person physically assaulted another. They are bleeding profusely from their left eye and cannot get off the floor.

Factors

  • The level of harm is categorised as medium to high
  • Our legal duty to protect may be being breached and therefore prosecution could occur
  • Leads to a person requiring medical treatment in hospital
  • Emergency services are required to respond and deal with incident – police and ambulance called
  • Leads to a safeguarding report where actual harm has occurred

# Aggression

Types: Threatening behaviour or language, verbal abuse, harassment (whether staff, person we support or visitor)

Example: Person intimidating towards another person and evokes fear in the other person.

Factors

  • The level of harm is categorised as medium to high
  • Our legal duty to protect may be being breached and therefore prosecution could occur
  • Leads to a person requiring medical treatment in hospital as they have frequent panic attacks as a result of the intimidation.
  • Leads to a safeguarding report where actual harm has occurred

# Safeguarding

Types: Abuse or suspected abuse, unprofessional behaviour, error in delivery of care

Example: Person claims another person touched them inappropriately

Factors

  • The level of harm is categorised as medium to high
  • Our legal duty to protect may be being breached and therefore prosecution could occur
  • May lead to the person requiring medical treatment in hospital
  • Emergency services are required to respond and deal with incident – police called
  • Leads to a safeguarding report where actual harm has occurred

# Damage or Malicious Acts

Types: Damage to property, theft, fire setting, criminal activity

Example: Person taunts another person by removing their favourite blanket every time they are on shift and returns it before others notice.

Factors

  • The level of harm is categorised as medium to high
  • Our legal duty to protect may be being breached and therefore prosecution could occur
  • Emergency services are Leads to a safeguarding report where actual harm has occurred

# Other

Types: Any other incident not listed above.

Example: Act of god- lightning strikes and causes all electrics to fail and service is without power for more than 24 hours.

Factors

  • May lead to a person or persons requiring medical treatment in hospital- e.g. severe cold and no heating causes hypothermia
  • Emergency services are required to respond and deal with incident.
