# OP05: Appendix 3 - Reducing Self Harm Guidelines

Appended to:

# OP05 - Programme Development

# Self-help strategies

# 1. Create a coping kit

Put positive and uplifting items in a shoebox or another container, which the person can use when they get the urge to self-harm. This can be anything from a journal to art supplies to upbeat music to photos of friends or family. Include anything they find calming or inspiring.

# 2. Model positive imagery

Visualizing a beautiful, serene place is a great way to reduce anxiety or painful emotions. When you practice positive imagery with the person you help them strengthen these skills. Talking aloud as you describe a soothing landscape – like a beach – or positive memories of a place you’ve been to. Use vivid details in your descriptions.

# 3. Talk about triggers

Helping better understanding of the types of situations and stressors that trigger their negative feelings can help prepare to consider the coping skills that will help the person manage triggers better. If it’s a social event or a dentist appointment or being on their own in the evenings are triggers, talk about how the days leading up to it can feel stressful and what me reduce the anxiety. This helps them to be prepared and have the necessary skills at their disposal. Also, talk about your personal triggers and the healthy ways you cope.

# 4. Suggest using less severe behaviours

If the urge to self-harm is still present, suggest using less severe activities, such as holding an ice cube, tearing paper, shredding a sheet, snapping a rubber band, sucking a lemon peel and pounding a pillow.

# 5. Suggest engaging in physical activities

The rush of adrenaline in physical activities, such as running, dancing and playing chase with their pet, produces the same chemical surge that self-injury does.

# 6. Be compassionate about setbacks

Stopping self-harming behaviour isn’t easy, and it’ll take time and people may have setbacks. The best approach if a setback occurs is to offer non-judgmental support. Research shows that shame, criticism, or overreaction when parents see a wound causes a person to withdraw back into self-harming behaviours.

# Some more suggestions from the Royal College of Psychiatrists

Trying to work out how you’re feeling…

1. Ask yourself “Do I feel angry?" "Do I feel anxious?" "What about?"
2. Ask yourself "What would the razor blade say if it could talk to me?"
3. Write a letter to someone you’re angry with (hurt by etc.) saying how you feel (No need to send it.)
4. Write a list of your achievements
5. Write a letter to yourself saying "I love you because…"
6. Make a list of things you’re thankful for
7. Make a wish list

Talking about it…

1. Talk to a friend
2. Call the Samaritans or other helpline
3. Allow yourself to cry (if you can)
4. Be creative
5. Draw / paint / collage / papier-mâché / finger paint / sculpt in clay - to express what you want to do or what you are feeling\ 6. Write a poem / story / song / joke / autobiography / parody / musical
7. Write a diary / journal or read old diaries (unless there might be triggers)
8. Write an online journal
9. Scribble a word again and again to say how you’re feeling e.g. "lonely", "angry"
10. Paint with red paint using your fingers
11. Take some photos
12. Play an instrument / Sing to music as loud as you can
13. Put on music which expresses how you are feeling
14. Write out the soundtrack to your life if it were a film
15. Imagine a colour which expresses your feelings then change it in your mind to another colour
16. Make a memory box / scrapbook
17. Write an alternative ending to a story
18. Watch a foreign language channel and make up your own interpretations
19. Create your own cartoon characters / legends
20. Create a secret code

Comforting yourself…

1. Have a bath or shower
2. stay in bed
3. Use aromatherapy oils
4. Eat chocolate (or whatever you want to eat)
5. Have an „emergency box‟ with whatever helps you cope
6. Buy something special
7. Massage your hands / arms / feet (or the area you want to harm)
8. Stroke a pet / cuddle a teddy
9. Ask a friend to hold you
10. Paint your nails / Have your hair done
11. Have a cup of tea
12. Rock / hug yourself
13. Give yourself a henna tattoo
14. Meditate / yoga

Leisure Activities

1. Watch television / video / DVD
2. Play on a computer
3. Go on the internet
4. Learn a new skill (juggling / making balloon animals)
5. Do puzzles / play chess / make your own puzzles
6. Origami / make jewellery
7. Sew / knit
8. Go through a photo album
9. Design a dream house
10. Have a debate
11. Build a card house
12. Make a paper chain of the days it’s been since you last cut (add a new one every day)
13. Find out how to put 8 queens on a chessboard without any of them being able to kill each other (There are 92 possible ways to do this)
14. Make a T-shirt
15. Look for pictures in the clouds

Getting Out and About…

1. Sweep the path
2. Wash your car
3. Do some gardening
4. Go for a drive
5. Go to an arcade or playground
6. Make a kite/fly a kite
7. Get on a train and go to the sea
8. Look at constellations

Being Productive...

1. Catch up on DIY / housework
2. Cook/bake something
3. Have a clear out – give your old stuff to charity
4. Re-arrange your room/decorate
5. Read/study
6. Give your pets a bath
7. Volunteer somewhere
8. Join a class
9. Think about what you’d like to change about your life and make a plan

Reasoning with yourself

When you’re not feeling like self-harming, write a list of reasons to avoid self-harm. The list will be different for each person - it's whatever makes sense to you. Look at the list when you feel like harming yourself. It could include things like:

  • “I’ve managed for two weeks without harming. I don’t want to start again”
  • “Once I start it’s difficult to stop”
  • “I’ll regret the damage afterwards”
  • “It doesn’t help in the long run. I can harm now but I’ll need to do it again in a couple of days.”
  • “I don’t want to end up in hospital”
  • “If I can hang on the need to self-harm sometimes passes”
  • “There may be consequences I don’t want e.g. brain damage, paralysis”
  • “Self-harm affects my relationships”

Learn CBT (Cognitive Behavioural Therapy) or DBT (Dialectical Behaviour Therapy) techniques. https://www.rcpsych.ac.uk/mentalhealthinformation.aspx

When you’re feeling good write yourself a letter you can read when you want to self harm, write down why you do not deserve to be hurt.

Making yourself safe

1. Try to identify things that prompt you to self-harm. If possible avoid them or prepare for them
2. Avoid shops that sell things you might use to harm yourself
3. Stay with a friend
4. Ask GP to give you weekly prescriptions or pick up medication for two days at a time from the chemist
5. Do not stockpile medication (prescribed or over the counter)\

Reducing stress

1. Do some relaxation exercises / listen to a relaxation tape
2. Ask a friend to look after the children for a few hours
3. Reduce your commitments in the next few days\

Rewarding yourself for not self-harming 1. Keep a chart – add a star for each day / hour you have not self-harmed
2. If you do self-harm, just leave a space and start again\

# Self Harm HELP LINES

Don’t suffer in silence – there are help lines out there that can offer support…

Samaritans

Helpline 116 123

Confidential emotional support for anyone, 24 hours a day, 7 days a week.

You can also e-mail jo@samaritans.org for support, or write to "Chris" P.O. Box 9090, Stirling, FK8 2SA.

Some centres also offer face to face support, usually by appointment.

Bristol Crisis Service for Women (BCSW)

Helpline 0117 927 9600

National helpline for women in distress, especially women who self-harm. Open Friday and Saturday evenings 9 pm to 12.30 am and Sundays 6pm to 9pm.

Support Line

Helpline 01708 765 200

A confidential helpline providing emotional support to individuals of any age on any issue. Support line also has a data base of local services. Ring for helpline opening hours. You can also e-mail on info@supportline.org.uk

Saneline

Helpline: 0300 304 7000

Offers practical care and support to anybody affected by mental health problems. Open noon to 11 pm Monday to Friday, noon to 6 pm Saturday & Sunday.

No Panic

Helpline 0844 967 4848

Support for people who experience panic attacks, and those with phobias, obsessive compulsive disorder, general anxiety disorder and who are withdrawing from tranquillisers. (10 am-10 pm every day)

email: info@nopanic.org.uk, web: www.nopanic.org.uk

ChildLine

Call 0800 11 11

Provides a free and confidential 24/7 helpline for any worries about children and young people, including self-harm.

HOPElineUK

Helpline 0800 068 41 41

Text 0778 620 9697

Provides a telephone service to support anyone concerned that a young person they know may be at risk of suicide.

email: pat@papyrus-uk.org

CALM

Helpline: 0800 58 58 58

Offers support to men of any age to prevent suicide.
